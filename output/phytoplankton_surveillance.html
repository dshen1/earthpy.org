Phytoplankton surveillance¶

Ilya Bolkhovsky <ilya@rshu.ru>
SOLab¶

Every time oceanographers explore our planet - from ship either from space - we see its unique beauty. Exciting ocean patterns and spectacular large-scale phenomena convince me again and again that there is no better artist or designer than our nature. One of my favorite examples of such artwork is a phytoplankton bloom in the Barents Sea.

Let's bring some explanation of these beautiful patterns from the MODIS website:

Like a peacock spreading its tail, the Barents Sea dazzled with color in late August 2012. The swirls of blue, green, and teal were created by a phytoplankton blooma profusion of microscopic, plant-like organisms thriving in the seawater. The Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) on NASAs Aqua satellite acquired this natural-color image on August 24, 2012.

Coccolithophores, a type of phytoplankton which flourish in nutrient-poor, sub-polar waters, have unique limestone (calcite) scales. This white coating makes the plant highly reflective, and thus a bloom can appear to be a bright, almost iridescent blue. The chalky coating can also cause whitish swirls in the water, making the blues washed out with a milky hue.

So how these phenomena expressing themselves in ocean measurements? What we can learn from data?

I will use matplotlib to build a map of a phytoplankton concentration. The plankton_source variable represents the 4-D array of phytoplankton concentration with time, depth and lat/lon dimensions. Here we are not taking into account the z-axis (depth) and visualize only the surface layer. Iterating over the time variable we will build a continuous set of images over the year 2012.

There is no something we did not expect: middle of summer is a peak for the phytoplankton bloom. Hovewer this is not always the same for internal seas due different salinity, rivers runoff and other reasons causing plankton species diversity. Thus it is known that a peak of a bloom for the Baltic Sea falls on March - April. It can be easily checked by reducing the same dataset with the boundaries of the Baltic Sea.

Results are quite surprising: despite of expected presence of April's peak we should also see an extensive bloom at the summer time. Obviously, it is a good starting point to re-validate these calculations and maybe to compare with other datasets and time ranges.

Conclusions¶

This small set of exersises aimed to show the beauty of the ocean and to highlight one of hundreds phenomena being studying by oceanographers. Phytoplankton plays a major role in the ocean food chain and it is prone to be easily damaged by pollutions. Going back to the optical pictures of the Barents Sea I would like to conclude with the following quote:

The distribution of phytoplankton is largely controlled by the polar front, ice cover, freshwater runoff and ice melting. Each water source the Artic, the Atlantic and the coastal water all bring their own characteristic species into the Barents Sea, creating a multi-specie and multi-color spectacle.

Because phytoplankton are the base of the marine food chain, places were blooms are large and frequent often support a thriving marine population. This is certainly the case in the Barents Sea where the fisheries, particularly the cod fisheries, are of great importance for both Norway and Russia.
